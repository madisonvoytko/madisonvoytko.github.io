Research

02

Osteoarthritis (OA) affects over 88% of adults aged 45 and older, making it one of the fastest-growing causes of disability worldwide. Current experimental models, including 2D cell cultures, animal studies, and 3D explants, fail to capture the mechanical environment that drives cartilage-bone interactions and OA progression.

My research tackles this challenge by engineering a mechanically tunable joint-on-chip platform that combines microfluidics, pressure-driven actuation, and multi-tissue interfaces. This system enables the study of osteochondral biology under human-relevant mechanical stimulation, providing a controlled and reproducible framework for investigating disease progression and therapeutic testing.

Research Process

Device Design

Multi-layer PDMS chip architecture incorporating separate bone and cartilage media channels, with shear chambers (blue), a compression chamber (dark blue), and a tissue chamber (bottom). Designed in SolidWorks to replicate joint articulation mechanics.

Fabrication Process

Plasma treating PDMS layers for bonding and membrane integration. Sterile fabrication protocols ensure device integrity and prevent contamination for future tissue studies.

Device Array

Six fabricated devices demonstrating reproducible manufacturing. Pink coloration indicates a rhodamine-dyed membrane used to facilitate ImageJ analysis. Each device underwent mechanical characterization to validate consistent performance.

Key Findings: Mechanical Validation

Bulk shear characterization demonstrated reproducible membrane deformation across 12 experimental trials (4 chips, 3 trials each). The pressure-driven actuation system generated consistent lateral displacement with less than 5% device-to-device variance, validating the platform's mechanical reliability for future tissue studies.

Testing Protocol: 0.2 mL air volume adjustments every 15 seconds, producing 0.8 mL total displacement

Imaging: Confocal microscopy (10X objective) with ImageJ/FIJI displacement analysis

Results: Uniform membrane deformation without pressure spikes, enabling consistent shear generation

Experience

03

August 2024 - Present

Undergraduate Researcher

Neu Research Lab, CU Boulder

Building mechanical systems to study how joints break down under load—and how to stop it.

The challenge: create a device that can mechanically load tiny pieces of cartilage and bone the same way your knee does when you walk, but small enough to fit under a microscope. I designed a pressure-driven system using layered silicone chambers that push and pull on tissue samples in real time.

After building and testing a dozen devices, we've proven the system works consistently—each one behaves almost identically, which is critical for reliable experiments. Now I'm building computer models to predict exactly how much stress the tissue experiences, so we can fine-tune the design before moving to human samples this spring.

August 2024 - Present

Test & Systems Engineer

3D-Printed Brain Phantom Project (CaliberMRI Partnership)

Making sure a 3D-printed brain phantom can survive the real world—and actually work in an MRI machine.

Our team partnered with CaliberMRI to develop a biomimetic brain phantom for MRI calibration. My job was to figure out where and how it would fail. I designed test protocols to push the device to its limits—checking if seals would leak, if thin walls would collapse, if the whole thing would hold up under handling and shipping.

I documented every potential failure mode, ranked them by risk, and presented findings to the CaliberMRI team so they could make informed design decisions. It's the unglamorous work that keeps medical devices from falling apart when they matter most.
